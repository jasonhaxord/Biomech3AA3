Teaching Biomechanics

Biomechanics of human motion is the study of the effects and control of forces that act on and are produced by the human body. The central word is "forces" which are the fundamental causes of motion and reminds us that we need to be concerned with kinetics (causes) as well as kinematics (descriptions). The forces that "act on" the human body are external forces such as gravity and the reactions of the ground and other objects. Forces that are "produced by" the human body are internal forces such as muscle, ligament and joint forces. The "control" aspect to the definition reminds us that we need to be concerned with neuromuscular control.

The field of biomechanics is multi-disciplinary and draws upon aspects of engineering, anatomy, mathematics, physiology, motor control, physics and many other disciplines. While it is not necessary to have degrees in each of these areas to practice biomechanics, tools from many of these areas are required. It is the objective of this page to teach the necessary skills and to provide a method of acquiring the necessary tools to gain expertise and to practice biomechanics effectively. It is anticipated that people from very different backgrounds will be using this method and so it has been designed to require very little previous knowledge of mathematics, physics and engineering. Indeed, those with advanced training in these areas are advised to move directly to the advanced topics.  The applications of biomechanics are also quite varied and a review of the goals and objectives of those who practice biomechanics can be found here.

My teaching philosophy is described in detail here. Briefly, practitioners of biomechanics need quantitative skills because biomechanics is a quantitative field. A strong understanding of biomechanical principles is reflected by the ability to solve applied problems. The ability to solve applied problems is achieved via clear instruction during lectures and via practice with numerous problems. This teaching section includes numerous lectures that mimic my teaching style in the lecture theater and when required, each lecture is followed by a set of practice problems that is delivered via the Smart Tutor method.

The lectures are grouped according to my requirements of undergraduate students studying kinesiology, physical education, exercise science, human kinetics, life science, etc. Biomechanics I and II are groups of lectures taken over two semesters that should be required of all undergraduate kinesiology students. Biomechanics III is a group of lectures that should be taken for elective credit by undergraduate students aiming for graduate or professional study such as prosthetics and orthotics, physical and occupational therapy, ergonomics, orthopaedics, chiropractics, etc.

Since other instructors and programs of study will have different requirements, the list of lectures has been arranged to allow visitors to pick and choose lectures in any order (i.e. ignore the groupings). However, each lecture assumes that a good understanding of the previous lectures has been achieved, so instructors are advised to go through each lecture and determine its appropriateness before assigning questions to students. Similarly, students using these lectures should be prepared to go back to earlier lectures that were skipped if the need arises.

The first laboratory topics have been chosen to review the philosophy of science and teach the scientific method that may have been taught in more general courses but it serves to reinforce the value of kinetics over kinematics and to remind students of the uncertainty involved in any scientific discipline.  This uncertainty can be easily lost with the early biomechanics lectures that have exact solutions to problems.  Other labs are designed to introduce students to the methods of acquiring and processing the data that are used in the lectures.  Like the lectures, the labs have been arranged to parallel the lectures but can be done in any order the instructor chooses.
