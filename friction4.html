Belt Friction:

An important use of friction is to wrap a rope or belt around an object to assist lowering or prevent motion of a heavy load. The formula below can be used to calculate the resistance or coefficient of friction in such cases.

Notice that in the figure above, T2 is equal to T1 if the coefficient of friction is zero or the angle (beta) is zero. The symbol µ represents the coefficient of friction and the angle beta represents the amount the belt is wrapped around the object. Beta is measured in radians and µ is also a unitless number. If the coefficient of friction is not zero and the angle is not zero, T2 is larger than T1 which means that friction is opposing motion caused by T2.

Example: Find the force required to keep the climber in static equilibrium given a mass of 80 kg, a coefficient of friction of 0.3 and a beta of 4 pi radians.

Solution:

T2 = T1 e (0.3)(4)(3.1416)

T2 = 43.38T1

Since T2 = 80(9.81) then T1 = 80(9.81)/43.38 = 18.09 N

Example: What is the coefficient of friction between the branch and the rope?

Given:

T2 = 800 N, T1 = 200 N

rope contacts half of the branch (180 degrees)

Solution:

ln(800/200) = µ(180/57.3)

                    µ = 0.441 

If the coefficient of friction is 0.441 how much force (T1) would be needed if he were to wrap the rope one and half times around the branch?

Solution:

800 / T1 = e(0.441)(1.5)(360)/57.3 

           T1 = 800/63.82 = 12.53 N

 

 

 

Final comment on the application of friction: 

 Friction is a force that always opposes motion. We have some equations that help us calculate the friction forces using the coefficient of friction. The coefficient of friction is a unitless number and we have some typical values for different contacting materials. However, in practice, the actual coefficient of friction often depends on a complex combination of factors that influence the interaction of the two contacting surfaces. These factors include the molecular interaction of the two bodies which can have adhesive properties, electrostatic and/or Van der Waals and hydrogen bonding forces, and other factors such as surface contamination with water or other substances. The determination of the friction coefficient often requires physical testing rather than a mathematical calculation with an assumed value for the coefficient of friction.

The image to the right shows a device called a "tribometer" that is used to measure the coefficient of friction of a floor surface. Such data is important in litigation of "slip and fall" injuries and setting safety standards for floor materials.
